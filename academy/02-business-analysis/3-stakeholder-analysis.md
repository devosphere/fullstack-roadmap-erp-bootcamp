# Stakeholder Analysis

**Version:** 1.0

---

# Purpose

Stakeholder Analysis is the process of identifying everyone who has an interest in the project, understanding their responsibilities, expectations, influence, and impact on the project's success.

Successful software projects are not built solely by developers—they are built through collaboration between business stakeholders, product teams, engineers, quality assurance, operations, and end users.

Throughout this bootcamp, every ERP module should begin by identifying its stakeholders before requirements are gathered.

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Identify project stakeholders
- Understand stakeholder responsibilities
- Analyze stakeholder influence and interest
- Prioritize stakeholder communication
- Manage stakeholder expectations
- Produce a professional Stakeholder Analysis document

---

# What is a Stakeholder?

A stakeholder is any person, group, or organization that can influence, is affected by, or has an interest in the project.

Examples include:

- Business owners
- Product Owners
- End users
- Managers
- Software Engineers
- QA Engineers
- DevOps Engineers
- Customers
- Third-party vendors
- Government agencies

Not every stakeholder interacts directly with the software, but each may influence project decisions.

---

# Why Stakeholder Analysis Matters

Understanding stakeholders helps teams:

- Gather accurate requirements
- Reduce project risks
- Improve communication
- Prioritize work effectively
- Resolve conflicts early
- Increase user adoption
- Deliver greater business value

Ignoring key stakeholders often leads to incomplete requirements and costly rework.

# Types of Stakeholders

## Business Stakeholders

Responsible for defining business goals and measuring project success.

Examples:

- Executive Sponsor
- Business Owner
- Department Head
- Finance Manager

---

## Product Stakeholders

Responsible for defining and prioritizing product requirements.

Examples:

- Product Owner
- Product Manager
- Business Analyst

---

## Technical Stakeholders

Responsible for designing, building, testing, and operating the system.

Examples:

- Solution Architect
- Backend Engineer
- Frontend Engineer
- QA Engineer
- DevOps Engineer
- Database Administrator

---

## Operational Stakeholders

Responsible for maintaining and supporting the system after deployment.

Examples:

- IT Operations
- Support Team
- Site Reliability Engineer (SRE)

---

## End Users

Individuals who use the application to perform daily business tasks.

Examples:

- Employees
- Managers
- HR Personnel
- Sales Representatives
- Warehouse Staff
- Customers

End users are often the most important stakeholders because they determine whether the solution is successful in practice.

# Stakeholder Identification

For each stakeholder, identify:

- Name or role
- Department
- Responsibilities
- Business goals
- Influence
- Interest
- Communication needs

Example:

| Stakeholder | Role | Responsibility |
|--------------|------|----------------|
| HR Manager | Process Owner | Defines leave management policies |
| Employees | End Users | Submit leave requests |
| Department Managers | Approvers | Review leave requests |
| Product Owner | Product Lead | Prioritizes requirements |
| Engineering Team | Development | Builds the solution |
| QA Team | Quality Assurance | Verifies software quality |

# Stakeholder Influence vs Interest Matrix

A useful way to prioritize stakeholder engagement is by evaluating each stakeholder's level of influence and interest.

```text
                     High Influence
                           ▲
                           │
        Manage Closely      │      Keep Satisfied
                           │
High Interest ─────────────┼──────────────► Low Interest
                           │
        Keep Informed       │      Monitor
                           │
                           ▼
                     Low Influence
```

Each stakeholder falls into one of the following categories.

---

## Manage Closely

High Influence, High Interest

These stakeholders make key decisions and should be involved throughout the project.

Examples:

- Executive Sponsor
- Product Owner
- Business Owner

---

## Keep Satisfied

High Influence, Low Interest

These stakeholders should receive regular updates but may not require involvement in day-to-day decisions.

Examples:

- Chief Executive Officer
- Chief Technology Officer
- Finance Director

---

## Keep Informed

Low Influence, High Interest

These stakeholders use the system regularly and should be informed of progress.

Examples:

- Employees
- Department Managers
- Customer Support Team

---

## Monitor

Low Influence, Low Interest

These stakeholders require minimal communication but should not be ignored.

Examples:

- External Auditors
- Vendors
- Contractors

# Stakeholder Responsibilities

Clearly define what each stakeholder contributes to the project.

Example:

| Stakeholder | Responsibilities |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Business Owner | Defines business objectives |
| Product Owner | Prioritizes the product backlog |
| Business Analyst | Gathers and documents requirements |
| Solution Architect | Designs system architecture |
| Software Engineers | Build application features |
| QA Engineers | Verify quality and functionality |
| DevOps Engineers | Manage CI/CD and deployments |
| End Users | Validate usability and business value |

# Communication Plan

Different stakeholders require different levels of communication.

Example:

| Stakeholder | Communication | Frequency |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| Executive Sponsor | Project Status Report | Monthly |
| Product Owner | Sprint Review | Every Sprint |
| Engineering Team | Daily Stand-up | Daily |
| QA Team | Test Planning | Every Sprint |
| End Users | User Acceptance Testing | Before Release |

A communication plan ensures stakeholders receive the information they need at the right time.

---

# Managing Stakeholder Expectations

Stakeholder expectations should be realistic and aligned with the project's scope.

Best practices include:

- Clarify project objectives early.
- Define project scope.
- Communicate progress regularly.
- Escalate risks promptly.
- Confirm requirements before implementation.
- Manage change requests through the product backlog.

Consistent communication builds trust and reduces misunderstandings.

# Common Mistakes

Avoid:

- Forgetting key stakeholders.
- Assuming all stakeholders have the same priorities.
- Ignoring end users.
- Failing to communicate project changes.
- Allowing stakeholders to bypass the agreed product backlog process.
- Treating stakeholder analysis as a one-time activity.

Stakeholder relationships should be reviewed throughout the project lifecycle.
